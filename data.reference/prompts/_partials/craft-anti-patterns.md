## Craft rules — anti-patterns to avoid

These are hard-won lessons about prose that reads as machine-generated. Follow them while drafting; a first draft that respects them needs far less editorial repair.

- **No triadic sensory lists.** Avoid the "X. Y. Z." rhythm of three parallel sensory fragments in a row (e.g. "The rain fell. The wind howled. The cold bit."). Combine two of them and cut the third.
- **"did not [verb]" ≤ once per chapter.** Negated-action constructions ("she did not move," "he did not answer") are a crutch. Prefer showing what the character *does* instead. Allow at most one per chapter.
- **No "thought about X" constructions.** Never write "she thought about the letter" or "he considered his options." Replace the reported thought with the thought itself (rendered directly), an action that reveals it, or a line of dialogue.
- **"the way X did Y" similes ≤ twice per chapter.** The "…the way the light did…" / "…the way she always did…" simile pattern reads as filler. Cap it at two per chapter; earn each one.
- **No over-explaining after showing.** If a scene demonstrates something — a character's fear, a relationship's tension, a stakes shift — do not have the narrator restate it afterward. Trust the demonstration and move on.
- **Section breaks ≤ two per chapter.** Hard section breaks (blank-line / horizontal-rule scene cuts within a chapter) fragment momentum. Use at most two per chapter.
- **Vary paragraph length.** Never write three or more consecutive paragraphs of similar length. Break the pattern deliberately — a one-line paragraph after two dense ones, or vice versa.
- **End each chapter differently from previous chapters.** Do not fall into a repeated closing rhythm (every chapter ending on a line of dialogue, or a lone-image beat, or an em-dash). Vary how endings land across the work.
- **At least 70% in-scene, not summary.** The majority of the prose should be dramatized moment-to-moment scene, not narrated summary ("Over the next week, they…"). Summary is for compression between scenes, not the default mode.
- **Dialogue sounds like speech, not polished prose.** People interrupt, trail off, use contractions and fragments, dodge the question. Do not write dialogue that reads like edited essay prose.
- **Include at least one genuinely surprising moment.** Every chapter should contain at least one beat the reader could not have predicted a paragraph earlier — a reveal, a reversal, an unexpected reaction. Predictable-throughout is a failure state.

## Stability Trap countermeasures

AI-drafted stories drift toward *stability*: characters resist real change, conflicts resolve too cleanly, nothing is truly lost, everything is revealed at once, and the emotional register sits at "room temperature." Actively push against this:

- **Characters must end TRULY different.** By the end of the chapter/issue, at least one character is meaningfully changed — a belief broken, a relationship altered, a line crossed. A character who ends exactly where they began is a warning sign.
- **Let bad things stay bad.** Not every wound gets bandaged. Resist the reflex to soften, walk back, or console away a hard beat before the scene ends.
- **Allow irreversible loss.** Real stakes require that some things cannot be undone — a death, a betrayal, a burned bridge. At least one consequence in the arc should be permanent.
- **Withhold information (information economy).** Do not reveal everything the moment it becomes relevant. Hold back what the character or reader doesn't yet need; let questions sit unanswered across scenes.
- **A choice with no real cost is not a real choice.** Every meaningful decision must cost the character something they wanted. If a choice is free, raise the price or cut it.
